## â“ Likely Q&A (Prepare Answers)

### Q1: "How do you know this will actually increase adherence?"

**Answer:**
> "We're basing this on peer-reviewed research:
> - Chai et al. 2022: Virtual pets increase adherence by 64%
> - HabitApp (Frontiers 2020): Gamification reduces anxiety by 40%
> - Multiple studies show non-punitive systems are more effective
> 
> But we also know that one-off games don't work. That's why we designed for a 6-month journey, not a quick fix.
> Yasmine's motivation needs to be sustained through all 36 sessions, not just the first few."

### Q2: "What about children who can't engage? (too sick, too sad)"

**Answer:**
> "Great question. That's why we have Rest Mode.
> If Yasmine is too sick or too tired, the pet 'rests' too. There's no punishment.
> The app is always there when she's ready.
> And the daily missions are suggestions, not requirements.
> We're meeting children where they are, not expecting them to perform at 100% every day."

### Q3: "How does this integrate with hospital systems?"

**Answer:**
> "In the MVP, it's a standalone app. But the technical architecture allows for:
> - API integration with EHR systems (hospital can mark sessions as completed)
> - Parent-facing dashboard (doctors can see adherence data)
> - Privacy by design (GDPR/HIPAA compliant)
> 
> For the hackathon, we've focused on the user experience. Backend integration would be Phase 2."

### Q4: "Won't children get bored of the pet after a while?"

**Answer:**
> "Not if the pet is evolving. Nour grows, gets new abilities, unlocks accessories.
> The pet becomes a 'mirror' of the child's own journey.
> Plus, the family messages change daily. The narrative progresses. Each day is fresh.
> 
> But if a child *does* get bored of Nour? They can switch pets at onboarding.
> Dragon, unicorn, robot, phoenixâ€”different aesthetics for different preferences."

### Q5: "Is this ethical? Don't games manipulate children?"

**Answer:**
> "That's the core of our design philosophy. Yes, most games use dark patterns.
> But games don't *have* to.
> 
> Hero's Journey uses game mechanics (progression, rewards, narrative) but avoids dark patterns:
> - No streaks that break and cause guilt
> - No notifications trying to pull them back in
> - No comparison or competition
> - No FOMO (fear of missing out)
> 
> It's ethical because it respects the child's emotional vulnerability."

### Q6: "What's the revenue model?"

**Answer:**
> "For a hackathon MVP, that's not the focus. But for a real product:
> - **B2B:** Hospital licensing (departments, health systems)
> - **B2C:** Freemium model for families (core app free, premium family features)
> - **Research:** Anonymized data for medical research (with consent)
> - **NOT:** In-app purchases, ads, or selling user data
> 
> The app must stay affordable/free for families in financial crisis."

### Q7: "How did you choose 36 sessions as the goal?"

**Answer:**
> "Acute leukemia treatment averages 6 months, which is about 36 chemotherapy sessions.
> That's our reference user, Yasmine's real timeline.
> 
> For other conditions:
> - Long-term cancer: 12-24 months (could be 50+ sessions)
> - Chronic disease: Could adapt to daily medications
> 
> But 36 is a psychologically meaningful number:
> - It's concrete and finite (not 'forever')
> - It has natural milestones (1/3, 1/2, 2/3)
> - It shows clear progress daily"

### Q8: "Where's the backend/database?"

**Answer:**
> "Good question. For this hackathon:
> - Frontend is fully functional
> - Backend is mocked/hardcoded (demonstrates concept)
> 
> For Phase 2 (real product):
> - PostgreSQL for user data, progress
> - Firebase for real-time family messages
> - Secure auth and medical data compliance
> - Parent dashboard with analytics
> 
> The architecture supports itâ€”we just focused on design and UX for the challenge."
